Learning Objectives · What you'll be able to do

By the end of this week you will…

01

Hold the client first in every contracted relationship.

Apply Codes 3.01–3.16 to service agreements, third-party contracts, referrals, continuity of services, and the transitions that most behavior analysts under-appreciate. Recognize when a stakeholder structure makes it harder to keep the client's interest primary — and what 3.04, 3.08, and 3.14 ask of you when that happens.

02

Supervise ethically across contexts.

Apply Codes 4.01–4.12 to fieldwork supervision, RBT supervision, and school-based supervision. Recognize when the structure of supervision itself is ethically compromised — including by your own organization. Take 4.04 (Accountability) seriously: your supervisees' decisions are your responsibility.

03

Distinguish ethical from unethical public statements.

Apply Codes 5.01–5.11 to testimonials, social media, advertising, and the gray space in between. Identify what counts as a public statement — including reposts, likes, and content where the BCBA is not the original author.

04

Adapt practice to digital modalities.

Reason about telehealth, AI-assisted clinical work, and video documentation under a Code that was written before most of these tools were in widespread use. Identify what the 2022 Code does and doesn't say — and what your professional judgment must fill in.

05

Take a defensible position on restraint.

Articulate when (if ever) physical restraint is consistent with the Code, what safeguards are non-negotiable, and how to respond when an organization or stakeholder requests restraint as a routine intervention. The point is not to memorize a policy — it's to defend yours.

Reading · 01
Sellers, Valentino, & LeBlanc (2016) — Recommended Practices for Individual Supervision of Aspiring Behavior Analysts
BAP · ~12 pp · open access

The foundational recommendations paper on supervision in behavior-analytic practice. Read it for the structural recommendations (caseload size, contact frequency, evaluation procedures) and the parts that have aged well versus less well in the eight years since publication. Pair it mentally with the 2022 Code as you read.

As you read, hold these in mind
  • Identify three structural recommendations the authors make. Which is hardest to implement in your setting?
  • Where, if at all, has the field's practice diverged from these recommendations since 2016?
  • Translate one recommendation into a concrete supervision procedure you could implement in your next supervision meeting.

Reading · 03
Cox, Jennings, & Syed (2024) — Starting the Conversation Around the Ethical Use of Artificial Intelligence in ABA
Behavior Analysis in Practice · ~14 pp · open access

The first behavior-analytic commentary on the use of large language models, generative AI, and other AI-assisted tools in clinical work — session note drafting, BIP generation, supervisor-supervisee feedback. Reads against the 2022 Code clause by clause. The literature here is thin and moving fast; expect this reading to be replaced annually.

As you read, hold these in mind
  • Identify three risks the authors name in AI-assisted clinical work.
  • What disclosure obligations do they argue are mandatory for BCBAs using AI tools? Where do you agree, and where would you push back?
  • Where does the 2022 Code already cover this (read carefully — you'll be surprised how much), and where does professional judgment have to fill the gap?

Assignment 01

Decision-Making Lab 3 — Supervision & Digital Practice

5  points

Apply the BACB 11-step decision-making model to the scenario below. Record your response as a Brightspace Discussion Video and post it to the Module 3 discussion thread.

⚜ Scenario

Maya, a BCaBA you supervise, has built a TikTok account titled @ABAwins with 4,800 followers. Posts are 30-second "before/after" clips with autistic clients. Faces are blurred but voices, settings, and parent-mentioned details are present. She tells you she runs the account on her own time, that "ABA needs better PR," and that the account is helping autistic creators "see what's possible." When you review her recent session notes, you find several were clearly drafted by ChatGPT — phrasing identical to AI output, including a hallucinated reference to a non-existent reinforcement schedule. The clinic's social media policy is two paragraphs written in 2019 and doesn't mention AI or short-form video. The clinic director — your boss — has been resharing Maya's TikToks on the clinic's official account. Yesterday, a parent of one of Maya's clients emailed you asking why their child appears in a video they don't remember signing a release for.

Your video must address

  1. A restatement of the facts and a list of all relevant stakeholders — including the clients in Maya's videos, who didn't choose to be there.
  2. The relevant Code citations — at minimum 4.01 (Supervision Compliance), 4.10 (Continuity of Supervision), 5.02 (Intellectual Property), 5.03 (Statements by Behavior Analysts), 5.04 (Statements by Others), 5.06 (Advertising), 2.05 (Rights of Clients), 2.06 (Confidentiality), and 3.04 (Service Agreement). Identify others that apply, especially around AI disclosure.
  3. Identification of the relevant Core Principles — paying particular attention to Maintain Competence and Behave with Integrity.
  4. At least three considered courses of action with anticipated consequences for each. One must address the upward dimension — your boss reposting Maya's content — not just Maya herself.
  5. Your selected action and a clear rationale grounded in the Code, the Core Principles, and the supervision/social-media literature from this week.
  6. A brief reflection on how you'd handle the AI-drafted session notes specifically — and whether your answer would change if Maya had disclosed the AI use upfront.

Format

5–7 minute Brightspace Discussion Video, recorded directly in the Brightspace discussion thread (no separate upload). Cite Code sections by number as you discuss them — verbal citations are fine. Visual aids (e.g., a slide showing the Code citations) are welcome but not required.

Assignment 02

Toolkit Artifact 3 — Social Media + Digital Practice Policy

2  points

Build the third artifact of your Practitioner Ethics Toolkit — a social media + digital practice policy you'd carry into supervision and bring to your next employer.

The policy must include

  • Personal accounts. What are you willing to post about your work, and what are you not? How does the answer change if your credential appears in your bio?
  • Client content. Photos, videos, anonymized stories, anonymized data — your rules for each. What requires written release? What never gets posted regardless of release?
  • Reposts and engagement. Your rule on liking, sharing, or commenting on parent-posted content about clients you serve.
  • AI tools. When and how you use AI-assisted drafting (session notes, BIPs, supervision feedback), what gets disclosed, and to whom. Include a disclosure-language template.
  • Supervision implications. How you'd train a supervisee on this policy in their first month, and what you'd do if you discovered them violating it.

Format

1 page, any layout. Will be graded on a Met / Approaching / Not Yet rubric. A starter template is available in Brightspace if you'd like a structure — feel free to design your own.

Assignment 03

Restraint Engagement — Position Video

3  points

After completing this week's resources and one additional restraint-specific reading or position statement of your choosing (BACB advisories, ASAN position papers, APBS guidelines, or an autistic-led perspective), post a Brightspace Video discussion (~2–3 minutes) addressing the three prompts below, and respond substantively to at least two classmates.

Discussion prompts

  1. Articulate your current professional position on physical restraint as a behavior-analytic intervention. Be specific — what are you for, what are you against, and where are you genuinely uncertain?
  2. Identify a real-world request you might receive (or have received) for restraint. What would your decision tree look like before you said yes, no, or "let me think"?
  3. Engage with the strongest disagreement to your position. Where might a thoughtful BCBA reasonably hold a different view, and how do you respond without dismissing them?

Note: This assignment is graded on the rigor of your reasoning and engagement, not on whether your position aligns with any particular school of thought. The point is to know where you stand and to defend it well.

Assignment 04 · Final

Ethical Scenarios Video Presentation — Final Submission

10  points

The capstone you've been developing since Module 1. Apply everything from Modules 1, 2, and 3 to your assigned ethical scenario. Record a 5–7 minute Brightspace Discussion Video presenting your full analysis, post it to the Final Scenarios discussion, and complete two peer reviews.

Your final video must include

  1. Scenario summary — a tight restatement of the facts, key stakeholders, and the central ethical question(s).
  2. Code analysis — the relevant Code citations across at least Sections 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, and 4.0 or 5.0. Show your work — this isn't a list, it's an argument.
  3. Core Principles framing — which of the four Core Principles are most in tension in your scenario, and how do you navigate the tension?
  4. Multiple courses of action — three or more, with consequences mapped for each. At least one must take seriously the perspective of the stakeholder whose voice is hardest to hear.
  5. Selected action with rationale — your decision, grounded in the Code, the Core Principles, and the autistic self-advocacy + digital practice frames from this course.
  6. Self-reflection — what about your own learning history, training, or biases shaped your analysis? What would you have missed without this course?

Peer review

After posting your video, review at least two classmates' submissions. For each, post a substantive reply (~150 words minimum) addressing: one place their analysis was strong, one place it could be strengthened, and one Code citation or Core Principle they didn't engage that you think was relevant. Peer reviews are graded as part of this assignment.

Format

5–7 minute Brightspace Discussion Video. Visual aids encouraged but not required. Cite Code sections by number as you reference them. The 10 points break down: 7 for the video analysis, 3 for the two peer reviews.
