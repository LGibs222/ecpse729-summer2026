Learning Objectives · What you'll be able to do

By the end of this week you will…

01

Apply Section 6.0 to research and quasi-research situations.

Reason about IRB, informed consent for research versus treatment, authorship, and conflicts of interest. Identify when clinical work has slid into research territory without the consent infrastructure to support it.

02

Navigate the BACB Code-Enforcement Procedures.

Know what gets reported, what happens after a complaint is filed, and what the actual range of consequences looks like. Distinguish what the BACB does from what practitioners assume the BACB does.

03

Identify patterns in real Notices of Disciplinary Action.

Read NDAs across categories. Identify the violations that come up most frequently, the ones that rarely show up but should, and what the pattern says about where the field's actual ethical risks live.

04

Synthesize a personal ethical practice.

Translate four weeks of Code analysis into a Code of Practice that's yours — what you commit to, what you refuse, and how you'll handle the gray cases the Code will never fully resolve.

Reading · 01
BACB — Annual Summary of Ethics Violations (most recent year)
bacb.com · ~15 pp · free PDF

The BACB publishes an annual summary of disciplinary actions — frequency by category, trends year-over-year, and de-identified summaries of what behavior actually triggered enforcement. This is the most honest picture of where the field's risks live. Read it cover to cover.

As you read, hold these in mind
  • What are the three most frequent categories of violations, and how does that compare to what you would have predicted before reading?
  • Identify one trend the report flags that seems to be growing year-over-year. What might explain it?
  • Where does your own practice or supervisory context have the most exposure to the categories in this report?

Reading · 02
Real Notices of Disciplinary Action — Pick Five Across Categories
bacb.com Notices · pick 5 · ~30 min

The BACB publishes individual Notices of Disciplinary Action — the redacted but specific records of what real BCBAs and BCaBAs got disciplined for. Go to the Notices page and select at least five recent NDAs across different violation categories. Read them carefully. These are the cases the field's lawyers, ethicists, and clinical directors discuss in the hallway. They're the cases you don't want to be in.

As you read, hold these in mind
  • For each NDA, identify the specific Code section(s) that were violated. Which sections show up across multiple NDAs?
  • What's the pattern in the consequences? When does the BACB issue a reprimand versus suspension versus revocation?
  • Pick the NDA that most surprised you — either because the violation was more severe than the consequence, or vice versa, or because it's a violation pattern you hadn't considered. Be ready to discuss in Assignment 03 (Disciplinary Patterns).

Assignment 01

Decision-Making Lab 4 — Research/Clinical Dual-Role Scenario

5  points

Apply the BACB 11-step decision-making model to the scenario below. Record your response as a Brightspace Discussion Video and post it to the Module 4 discussion thread.

⚜ Scenario

Dr. Chen is a BCBA-D and clinical director at a community-based ABA agency in Brooklyn. Over the past 18 months she ran a series of single-subject reversal designs with five clinical clients, testing a brief stimulus-pairing protocol she developed. The data are striking — large effects, clean reversals, a paper waiting to be written. She wants to submit it to JABA. The original parent intake forms include consent for treatment but no language about research participation or potential publication. Two of the five clients have aged out and the agency has lost contact with their families. She tells you she's planning to "retrospectively recast it as a clinical case series, since case studies don't require IRB." She also wants to add Maya — the BCaBA you supervise, who collected most of the data sessions — as second author. Maya wasn't told at the time that the data might be published. The agency director (your boss) is excited about the publication and has already mentioned it in a fundraising email to donors.

Your video must address

  1. A restatement of the facts and a list of all relevant stakeholders — including the absent families who can't speak for themselves, and the donors who've already been told.
  2. The relevant Code citations — at minimum 6.01 (Conforming with Laws and Regulations), 6.02 (Research Review), 6.03 (Research in Service Delivery), 6.05 (Informed Consent in Research), 6.10 (Authorship), 5.04 (Statements by Others), 4.01/4.07/4.10 (Maya supervision), 1.05/1.06 (clients' rights), 3.04 (Service Agreement), and 1.13 (Multiple Relationships). Identify others that apply.
  3. Identification of the relevant Core Principles — paying particular attention to Behave with Integrity and Maintain Competence.
  4. At least three considered courses of action with anticipated consequences for each. One must address what to do about the donor email that's already been sent.
  5. Your selected action and a clear rationale grounded in the Code, the Core Principles, and what you've learned across all four modules.
  6. A brief reflection: based on the NDA patterns you read this week, where would this scenario likely end up if it went forward as Dr. Chen proposes? Be specific about which Code sections would be cited and what consequence you'd expect.

Format

5–7 minute Brightspace Discussion Video, recorded directly in the Brightspace discussion thread (no separate upload). Cite Code sections by number as you discuss them — verbal citations are fine. Visual aids (e.g., a slide showing key citations) are welcome but not required.

Assignment 02 · Capstone

Toolkit Artifact 4 — Your Personal Code of Practice

10  points

The capstone document for this course. Translate four weeks of Code analysis, Decision Labs, and Toolkit artifacts into a Code of Practice that is yours — the document you'd hand a future employer, a fieldwork supervisee, or a parent who asks "what kind of BCBA are you, exactly?"

Your Code of Practice must include

  • A professional identity statement. Two-to-four sentences naming the kind of behavior analyst you commit to being. Specific. No platitudes.
  • Non-negotiables. What you will not do, regardless of pressure from supervisors, employers, parents, or schools. Tie at least three of these to specific Code sections, but state them in your own voice.
  • Daily practices. What you will always do — assent monitoring, documentation, reflective supervision, etc. — concrete enough that a colleague reading them could check whether you're keeping the commitments.
  • Your gray-case framework. When the Code is unclear or the right answer is contested, what's your decision-making heuristic? Reference the BACB 11-step model and adapt it to your own thinking.
  • One commitment from each module. One specific practice you've added to your repertoire because of Module 1 (foundations/CLD ethics), Module 2 (assent and self-advocacy), Module 3 (supervision and digital practice), and Module 4 (research and enforcement awareness). Name them concretely.
  • What you've changed your mind about. One position or assumption you held entering this course that you now hold differently — and why. This is the highest-leverage section. Don't skip it.

Format

1–2 pages, any layout. Will be graded on a Met / Approaching / Not Yet rubric across the six required components, with the "changed your mind" section weighted most heavily. A starter template is available in Brightspace if you'd like a structure — feel free to design your own. This document is for you. Use it.

Assignment 04

Practitioner Interview — A Real Ethical Situation

4  points

Conduct a 30–45 minute informational interview with a practicing BCBA — your supervisor, fieldwork mentor, an alumni you have access to, or any credentialed BCBA who has practiced for at least three years. The topic: a real ethical situation they navigated and what they learned from it.

The interview should explore

  • The situation. A specific ethical dilemma they faced. Real, not hypothetical. (You'll de-identify any client/colleague specifics in your write-up.)
  • The competing considerations. What made it hard? What stakeholders, Code sections, organizational pressures, or personal commitments were in tension?
  • The decision. What did they do, and how did they arrive there?
  • The aftermath. What happened? What did they learn? What would they do differently?
  • Their advice. What would they tell a new BCBA approaching a similar situation?

Submit two pieces

  1. A 1-page de-identified write-up summarizing the interview — situation, decision, aftermath, key takeaway. Use pseudonyms; do not include identifying details about clients, colleagues, or organizations.
  2. A ~2-minute Brightspace Video reflection on what surprised you most about the conversation and what you'd carry into your own practice from it.

Format

Interview can be in-person, by phone, or by video call. Consent of the interviewee is required. The interviewee receives a copy of your de-identified write-up before submission. The 4 points break down: 2 for the write-up, 2 for the video reflection.
